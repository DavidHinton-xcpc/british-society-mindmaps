# Chapter One: General Introduction

## 1.1 Geography

Situated in Northern Europe, lying to the north of France and the west of the Netherlands and Denmark, the United Kingdom of Great Britain and Northern Ireland (the UK for short) is made up of four parts known as England, Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland, whose capital cities respectively are London, Edinburgh, Cardiff and Belfast.

Each of the four parts demonstrates its own geographical characteristics in terms of land forms, of which the most famous ones are the Pennines, Lake District, Grampian Range, etc. The UK is also one country possessing many rivers, some of which provide irrigation, and few navigation. It is well worth mentioning that the Thames River and the Clyde River are respectively the most important rivers in England and Scotland. As an island country, Britain has a long but irregular coastline. The gentler eastern coastline consists mainly of limestone and chalk cliffs, among which the most famous are the White Cliffs of Dover. As for the British climate, 3 characteristics can be summarized. The first is uncertainty and changeability; the second is much precipitation, and the last one is mildness. Small as its territory is, the UK is relatively rich in such natural resources as coal, oil, natural gas, limestone, iron, tin and water resources as well.

The population of the United Kingdom is 60,209,500 (2005 estimates), England being the part with the largest population compared with the other 3 parts of the UK. English is the most widely spoken language in this country, but Gaelic and Welsh are also popular languages respectively in Scotland and Wales. In their daily lives and work, British people show the world four features of their personalities: arrogance, conservativeness, sense of humor and politeness.

Marriage is one of the most important issues for all British people. All children above the age of 18 have the freedom to get married regardless of their parents' consent. The wedding ceremony is usually held in the church, after which relatives and friends are invited to dinner. For British meals, besides the generally accepted breakfast, lunch and supper, British people have a unique and additional course called afternoon tea. There are a large number of holidays and festivals celebrated by British people all through the year and they can be divided into such 3 types as religious holidays, bank holidays and public holidays.

---

### 1.1.1 England

As the largest and most southerly portion of Britain, England shares land borders with Scotland to the north and Wales to the west; the Irish Sea is to the northwest, the Celtic Sea to the southwest and the North Sea to the east, with the English Channel to the south separating it from Continental Europe.

With a total area of over 130,000 square kilometers, England may be divided into three different regions, each of which bears its specific characteristics.

Rolling downs and low-lying plains are the major landforms of one of the three regions—the east and the southeast. What follows is the second region—middle England, a region of mountains. The most significant mountain range in this region is the Pennines, which, since the 19th century, has been called the "back bone of England". It runs from the Scottish border as far south as Derbyshire with the highest point Cross Fell which is 893 meters high. On the west side of the northern Pennines lies the Lake District (also called The Lakes or Lakeland), which is well known for its beautiful lakes and mountains as well. The Lake District National Park formed in 1951 is to protect the landscape by restricting unwelcome change from industry or commerce. It is in the central and most-visited part of the Lake District. Lake Poets, among whom the most famous are William Wordsworth, Samuel Taylor Coleridge and Robert Southey, lived here and found inspirations from the beautiful scenery.

---

### 1.1.2 Scotland

Occupying the northern third of the island of Great Britain, Scotland shares a border with England to the south and is bounded by the North Sea to the east, the Atlantic Ocean to the north and west, and the North Channel and Irish Sea to the southwest.

Geographically, Scotland can also be divided into 3 natural regions, namely, the northern Highlands, the central Lowlands (also called Midland Valley) and the southern Uplands.

The northern highlands, just as the name implies, is a mountainous, rugged and bare region with a sparse population. The Grampian Ranges lies peacefully in the highlands and the western part of the Range includes Ben Nevis, the highest peak in Britain with an altitude of 1,343 meters. Since the western highlands are close to the Atlantic Ocean, long and narrow sea lochs or fjords are formed in this area, attracting numerous tourists from across the world for sightseeing. South of the Highland boundary, the central Lowlands, which is recognized as the most important area in Scotland and contains most of the industry and population, is a region of hills and rolling country, with Edinburgh and Glasgow serving respectively as the capital city and the biggest city of Scotland. Still farther to the south bordering with England lies the southern Uplands, a region of rolling moorlands cut by some small fertile river valleys. The highest peak of this region is Cheviot Hills of about 816 meters.

---

### 1.1.3 Wales

Wales is bordered by England to the east and by sea in the other three directions: Bristol Channel to the south, Celtic Sea to the west, and the Irish Sea to the north, thus possessing a coastline as long as over 1,200 km.

With Cardiff as its capital city, Wales covers an area of approximately 20,700 square kilometers, which is less than 9% of the total territory of Britain. It is about 274 km north south and 97 km east west. Geographically speaking, Wales consists of 3 major parts called respectively the industrial south, the central plateaus and lakes, and mountainous north of farmers and tourists. Most of Wales is mountainous mainly formed by the Cambrian Range, whose highest peak is Snowdon in the north.

---

### 1.1.4 Northern Ireland

This part of the UK, covering an area of 14,147 square kilometers, is situated in the northeast of the island of Ireland and shares a border with the Republic of Ireland to the south and west. The land of Northern Ireland is in the shape of basin with mountains in the north and south, and plains in the center with Lough Neagh as the largest lake in the whole country. Agriculture is dominant in Northern Ireland while industry concentrates only in the two ports of Belfast and Londonderry.

## 1.2 Rivers and Coast

### 1.2.1 Rivers

Due to the topographical relief of Britain and the sufficient annual precipitation, a large number of rivers cover the whole country. Being short and swift, however, few rivers in Britain are navigable.

The longest river is the Severn River (354 km in length), which rises in the mountains of Wales and then discharges into the Bristol Channel which in turn discharges into the Celtic Sea and the wider Atlantic Ocean.

The second longest and most important river in Britain is the Thames River (338 km in length). It rises in southwest England and flows through such cities as London, Oxford, Reading and Windsor and out into the North Sea. It has a special significance in flowing through London, the capital of the United Kingdom, although London only includes a short part of its course. Flowing very slowly, the Thames River has always been quite favorable for water transportation. The water under the Tower Bridge in London is one of the most important and busiest commercial routes in the world. Other important rivers include the Clyde River in Scotland and the Trent River in England.

---

### 1.2.2 Coast

Great Britain's coastline is highly irregular, with many bays and inlets that provide harbors and shelters for ships and boats. As a matter of fact, coastal trade has long been carried on ever since ancient times.

The coastline is about 8,000 km long and affords some of the best scenery in Britain. The western coast is characterized by cliffs and rocky headlands, especially where the Highlands meet the sea in northwestern Scotland. On the more gentle southern and eastern coasts, there are many sand or pebble beaches as well as tall limestone or chalk cliffs, the most famous of which are the White Cliffs of Dover in the southeast. These cliffs form part of the British coastline facing France. They have great symbolic value for Britain because they face towards Continental Europe across the narrowest part of the English Channel, where invasions have historically threatened and against which the cliffs form a symbolic guard. Because crossing at Dover was the primary route to the Continent before air travel, the white line of cliffs also formed the first (or last) sight of the UK for travelers.

When referring to the British coast, we can never avoid the discussion of the narrow sea—the English Channel, which separates the UK from France, or to be exact, the whole European Continent. It is about 560 km long and varies in width from 240 km at its widest, to only 34 km in the Strait of Dover. It is the smallest of the shallow seas around the Continental shelf of Europe, covering an area of some 75,000 km². With the Channel Tunnel (Chunnel for short), both passenger and freight trains can travel freely through the English Channel, therefore, tourists can enjoy more convenient transportation and Britain is no longer an island after it has been connected with the European continent.

## 1.3 Climate

Generally speaking, the climate in Britain has three features.

The first one is uncertainty and changeability. There exist such two popular statements as "Other countries have a climate; in England we have weather" and "In no country other than England can one experience four seasons in a single day", which, to a certain extent, vividly show us the peculiar meteorological conditions of British climate. Day may break as a sunny and nice spring morning; an hour later or so black clouds may appear from nowhere and the rain may be pouring down. This explains why English gentlemen tend to go out with umbrellas in their hands even on shiny days.

The second feature is more rainy days but fewer sunny days. The rain drizzles all year round without any marked dry seasons. Annual rainfall ranges from 67 cm in the eastern and southeastern districts to over 254 cm in some northeastern mountainous areas.

The final feature of the British climate is its mildness. The climate of the United Kingdom is classified as an oceanic climate, with warm summers, cool winters and plentiful precipitation throughout the year. The principal factors that influence the country's climate include its northerly latitude (which ranges from 50° to 60° N), the close proximity to the Atlantic Ocean, and the warming of the surrounding waters by the Gulf Stream which make the climate there much milder than that of many places in the same latitude.

## 1.4 Natural Resources

Despite the fact that the United Kingdom is a country with a relatively small territory, yet it is rich in such natural resources as coal, oil, natural gas, limestone, iron, tin, etc., among which the most important are coal, oil and natural gas. Apart from these, water resources is also a fundamental constituent of all natural resources.

### 1.4.1 Coal

Britain has large deposits of coal, mined for more than 300 years. For most of the 19th and 20th centuries, coal was Britain's richest natural resource, meeting most of the nation's requirement for energy. The Ayrshire field in Scotland and deposits of the lowlands continue to supply the Scottish industry. The coal mining area of central England, including York, Birmingham and Nottingham, Leicester and Derby, is regarded as the largest one of the country, constituting approximately 2/5 of the total output. Durham, Northumberland and Canterbury in the northeast and southeast of England are also important coalmines.

However, coal production has declined rapidly since the 1970s. In 2000, only 35 million tons of coal was produced compared to 145 million in 1970.

### 1.4.2 Oil and Natural Gas

In the past, Britain had to totally depend on importing oil supplies so as to meet the needs of the industrial development. This kind of situation had never changed until some big oil fields were discovered under the North Sea, east of the British Isles, generally along the British-Norwegian boundary in 1965. Since that time on, Britain has gradually become self-sufficient in oil. The oil deposit is estimated as 3,000 to 4,500 million tons. Britain began to put oil into production in the 1970s and by 1980 the annual output of oil had reached 100 million tons.

Gas was also discovered under the North Sea during the 1960s and new supplies are still being found today. Gas has been particularly important in replacing coal as a fuel for generating electricity.

Oil and gas were formed from the remains of animals and plants that lived millions of years ago in a marine (water) environment before the dinosaurs. Over the years the remains of the plants and animals were covered by layers of mud and soil which eventually turned to rock, trapping the remains beneath the rock. Pressure and heat changed some of this organic material into coal, some into oil (petroleum), and some into natural gas—tiny bubbles of odorless gas.

### 1.4.3 Limestone, Iron, and Tin

Limestone is quite widespread in Britain, especially in northern and southern Pennines and north Wales as well. It is acknowledged that northern Derbyshire is the place where limestone of the highest quality comes from. The iron deposits in Britain used to be very rich before. Unfortunately, some have already been exhausted out and Britain is no longer one that can be self-sufficient in iron ore. More than half of its domestic consumption must be imported from abroad each year. Lincolnshire and Northamptonshire are the two districts where the present major iron deposits lie. Tin ore has been worked in Cornwall and Devon for more than 3,000 years and tin mining in this area has reached a depth of 3,300 feet (1,000 meters), providing about 40% of Britain's requirements of the metal.

### 1.4.4 Water Resources

Judging from the climate of Britain with a heavy precipitation and many existing short and rapid rivers, we may naturally come to the conclusion that Britain is quite rich in water resources. The free-flowing rivers of Britain provide abundant surface water, even though people there have to conserve with much care the supply of water especially in the face of a large population. In addition, large reservoirs provide a water supply in the Welsh mountains and some of the lakes in the Lake District. It is a surprising fact that about 38% of the country's water requirements are obtained from underground sources. In recent years, however, there has been an increasing degree of water pollution that are doing much harm to many English and Scottish rivers, and these are the environmental problems still to be conquered.

## 1.5 Population and Language

### 1.5.1 Population

The population of the United Kingdom is 60,209,500 (2005 estimates), which on the whole consists of the English (50 million, 83.8%), Scottish (5 million, 8.4%), Welsh (2.96 million, 4.9%) and Irish (1.7 million, 2.9%) and immigrants.

### 1.5.2 Language

There is no denying that English serves as the dominant language in the UK, and is spoken by most of the population. However, it is by no means the only language spoken all over the whole country. In Wales there are many people who speak Welsh, about one in four, to be exact. In Scotland, Gaelic is adopted by about 80,000 people living on the Highlands. In a word, currently there exist 3 major languages being adopted as a tool for communication in all walks of life of the UK. Dialects that were once heard in different parts of the country are heard less and less in movies, radios, television and other public media so as to promote the use of standard English. With the three co-existing major languages, many road signs, or signs that appear in such public places as train stations, restaurants and so on are bilingual ones.

## 1.6 British Characters

Different people have different qualities or characters and the British people are no exception to this rule. What's more, their characters are fairly vivid and unique as typically their own. Generally speaking, there are four characters that we can observe among the British people, namely, arrogance, conservativeness, sense of humor and politeness.

### 1.6.1 Arrogance

Most of the British people, the English people in particular, have a sense of superiority. They regard their ways of behavior are the best, the most reasonable compared to those from any other country of the world. Take the local newspapers for example. They probably tend to have more interest in covering domestic news rather than foreign because British populace is relatively less concerned about what's happening all around the globe. If there is any coverage about the outside of Britain, it is quite likely to be about Europe and America, etc., which has much to do with their national interests. This attitude shows that they are not interested in all those countries "inferior" to Britain. There are two reasons that can explain the arrogance of the British people, one of which is the special geographic location, while the other is its glorious history.

Britain is an island country which is cut off, separated and isolated from the rest of the world by the English Channel and the Atlantic Ocean. It is because of this special geographical location that British people tend to, when staying in Britain, refuse to regard their country as one part of Europe and in their mind, all the Europeans are foreigners. However, this attitude has changed dramatically since the opening of the Channel Tunnel in 1994.

British people have every reason to be proud of the history of their country, because there existed such a large number of well-known people and events in the history as King James Bible, Shakespeare's plays and sonnets (which have been exerting great influence on Western and global culture), the British Parliament (the oldest in Europe), the Industrial Revolution as early as the 18th century and so on and so forth.

### 1.6.2 Conservativeness

This character means that the British people have an acceptance of things that are familiar, and inclination to be suspicious of anything that is strange or foreign.

Whenever there is a new idea or invention coming from other countries like America, the British people might say, "Be careful. It can be quite dangerous!" One of the examples to illustrate this can be found in adopting rational reforms like the metric system. In spite of the inconvenience that they have always been suffering from sticking to the old ways, yet they refuse to be involved in the trouble of trying the new ones. It was after a long period that metrication of weights and measurements become relatively widely adopted in Britain, although in some shops measurements are still given in inches, feet and yards.

Another example is about British people's choice between traditional fireplaces and central heating. It is a fact that in the past all houses of Britain, including those in such big cities as London, Liverpool, Manchester and Birmingham, had fireplaces in living rooms to keep the family members warm especially in winter. However, when central heating was developed in the USA, British people still regarded fireplaces superior to the new invention, regardless of its effectiveness and convenience. They thought a room without a fireplace lacked the basic character of coziness. After the introduction of central heating, many new houses were built without fireplaces, but the British still prefer to have a fireplace even if it is not used.

### 1.6.3 Sense of Humor

Almost all British people have a strong sense of humor, which is generally self-deprecating. They tend to laugh at the mistakes made by themselves or their own weak points, failures and embarrassments. For instance, they usually complain about some awful aspects of British life. Their complaints may be as follows: trains never arriving on time, bureaucratic bungling that has driven citizens to commit suicide or food so disgusting that even a dog refuses to eat. However, the attitudes shown in the British humor are never cruel or disrespectful or even malicious for it is only an attitude towards life. As a result, you seldom see British people laughing at a cripple or a madman for their misfortune or tragedy.

### 1.6.4 Politeness

British people are also well known for their politeness which is based on the basic rule of showing consideration for others. An example good enough as proof is what usually happens in public, where everything is in order, making society a highly civilized one. In shops, bus or train stations, or the banks, people have been used to waiting in queues, with certain space kept between individuals. What's more, no matter how long the queue is or how long they have to wait, you can rarely hear any complaints; on the contrary, all people wait in the queue with great patience.

If British people do need some help from others, they prefer to start their request by saying "I know the trouble I am causing you, but would you mind?" After having been helped by others, immediately and spontaneously they express their sincere gratitude and thanks with all words possible.

## 1.7 British Family Life

### 1.7.1 Marriage

In terms of marriage, it is against the law for children under the age of 16 to get married even though they have got their parents' approval. Between the age of 16 and 18, getting married is legal but in the meantime it is still necessary for children to get their parents' consent first. However, when children are above the age of 18, parents' consent becomes no longer necessary. Despite the above fact, the majority of boys and girls tend to get married after they have reached the age of 18.

Marriage is considered to be one of the most significant events in British people's whole lives in that it marks the beginning of a new and independent family, which means leaving one's parents and starting one's own life for both the man and the woman. After having got married, new couples face a situation that their parents, brothers or sisters and other close relations have no hand in their personal affairs and new couples are the masters of themselves.

Young people in Britain do not like to have control placed upon them and their love affairs. That a girl at the age of 16 goes out with a boyfriend is considered quite normal and acceptable. Kisses and embraces between them are natural behavior to show their love to each other. If they have been together for a long time and have known each other very well, the girl would bring her boyfriend home to meet her parents, and so would the boy. The purpose of doing this is not merely making themselves familiar with the parents of one another, but more importantly, to ask their permission and hope for their congratulations and encouragement as well. It is, in fact, usually the young couple themselves who made privately the promise to marry. In that case, it has been a tradition that the boy should carry out the first step towards marriage—to buy an engagement ring and place it on the third finger of the girl's left hand, which is a formal declaration of love to the public, discouraging the possible attempts from other men. During the period between engagement and marriage, if the girl changes her mind, she tends to return that ring to the boy; on the contrary, if they continue getting along very well with each other, the engagement ring will be replaced by a wedding ring of plain gold.

For the wedding, many British couples, no matter whether they are religious or not, would choose going to church to hold the ceremony together with some friends and relations who come over for celebration activities. On the marriage day, the bride is usually dressed in a long white dress. The first step of the whole ceremony is that the vicar will ask them whether they are willing to marry the other. The answer, undoubtedly, is usually affirmative. They then have to make promises of loyalty to each other until they die. The bride is usually given away by a close relative to her husband, in most cases, by her father. This is an indication of the concept that marriage bond is stronger than any previous family bonds, stronger even than the bond between the bride and her mother. For the bride's mother, a daughter's wedding is both sad and joyful in that she shares her daughter's happiness, but she feels that she has lost her since the moment. What follows the ceremony is a reception of light meal to which all the relatives and friends are invited. This is paid by the bride's parents.

### 1.7.2 Meals

Meals are regarded as an important element of family life because this is a perfect time for all the family members to sit together around the table at relatively fixed time. A special attitude British people hold towards meals is that having meals is really private and formal, which means they don't really like to share with outsiders unless they make invitations in advance. As a result, it is impolite to visit a British family at meal time and if you happen to have done so, making an apology for your interruption is quite necessary. Generally speaking, British daily meals consist of such 4 specific steps as breakfast, lunch, afternoon tea and supper.

**(1) Breakfast.** The traditional English breakfast consists of eggs, bacon, sausages, fried bread, baked beans and mushrooms. Even though not many people will eat this for breakfast today, it is always served in hotels and guest houses around Britain. British people tend to choose a less substantial dish, such as kippers or boiled eggs, which is usually preceded by porridge, fruit juice, or some variety of commercially produced breakfast cereal, an early 20th-century innovation of US origin. However, recently, with the life pace getting faster, many British people have less breakfast and some even have no breakfast at all or only a hot drink.

**(2) Lunch.** Lunch time is from about 12:30 to 2:00 p.m. British lunch means different things to different people. For those men who work fairly far away from their homes and students studying in remote schools prefer a packed lunch. This typically consists of a sandwich, a packet of crisps, a piece of fruit and a drink. The "packed lunch" is kept in a plastic container. However, for those who don't have the above trouble, they would rather stay together with their family members around the dinner table for a formal lunch. The first course is usually a small quantity of soup. The main course consists of meat, potatoes, and other vegetables. The meat, more often than not, is beef or lamb cut into slices; potatoes are boiled or roasted in the oven, while vegetables are commonly cabbages, carrots, peas, beans, onions and cauliflower. What follows the main course is a light course called the dessert.

**(3) Afternoon tea and supper.** Tea-time is a small meal eaten in the late afternoon (usually between about 3:30 and 5:00). People may drink tea, and often eat biscuits, cakes or savory foods such as sandwiches, crumpets or tea-cakes.

High tea is a light meal eaten in the early evening served with a pot of tea. Supper is the most common name for the meal eaten in the evening (usually between 7:00 and 8:30). A dinner party is a formal evening meal to which guests have been invited. A common type of cooked meal is meat and two veg. This is a meat dish served together on the same plate with two types of vegetable, one of which is often a type of potato. It is common to eat a dessert (also known as a pudding, or afters) after the main dish.

It is increasingly popular for British people to get a takeaway or go to a restaurant instead of cooking at home, and often this is used as a chance to try different types of food. Most towns have an Indian restaurant and Chinese restaurants are also very common. Many people like Italian pizza and pasta dishes. Fast food restaurants often serve beef burgers or fried chicken. Fish and chip shops are still popular, especially in towns by the coast.

## 1.8 Holidays and Festivals

People in Britain celebrate a number of holidays throughout the year, among which are religious holidays like Christmas Day and Easter, Bank Holidays and some other public holidays like Easter Monday, the Boxing Day and the New Year's Day.

### 1.8.1 Religious Holidays

**(1) Christmas.** The word Christmas comes from the Old English name "Cristes Maesse" and is the celebration of the birth of Jesus. The first recorded observance occurred in Rome in AD 360, but it was not until AD 440 that the Christian Church fixed a celebration date of 25th December. Christmas in England began in AD 596, when St Augustine landed on her shores with monks who wanted to bring Christianity to the Anglo-Saxons. Christmas has always been the biggest and most attractive festival for both adults and children.

For British people, since Christmas falls in winter rather than in summer when they could get away somewhere, Christmas is basically a home and family festival. It is an excellent time for all the family members to get together no matter how far they live from each other. Purchasing Christmas gifts for friends and relations is so common during the festival that you would never be surprised to see big shops which are gaily decorated and brightly lit, stay open long after dark. A Christmas gift can be a card on which some words of best wishes are written or many other things that may amuse the addressee. Whatever the gift is, it must be beautifully wrapped.

A typical British tradition on Christmas is the Queen's Christmas Message made to the Commonwealth. The tradition began in 1932 with a radio broadcast by King George V on the BBC Empire Service. Today the broadcast is made on television and radio via various providers.

At Christmas, the home is usually decorated with colorful paper chains, leaves of holly and mistletoe, and attractive greeting cards received. The Christmas tree stands in the corner of the room, with the branches and leaves decorated with colorful lights and shining gifts. A big evening dinner provides the whole family not only with delicious food but also a moment for them to gather and communicate. Turkey, duck, goose, chicken, Christmas pudding, and mince pies are the most common food on a Christmas dinner table.

Father Christmas, though only an imaginary figure, has been attractive especially to children who expect wonderful presents from him. In children's opinion, Father Christmas is a merry old man with a long white beard and long coat trimmed with white fur, coming all the way from the cold Northern lands on a magic sleigh drawn by reindeer with jingling bells. Children believe that when they are asleep, Father Christmas comes down silently through the chimney and leaves them presents near the beds, which in fact have been bought and put into stockings by their parents.

**(2) Easter** is another religious holiday in Britain, which begins on Good Friday—the Friday before Easter and ends on Easter Sunday, celebrating Christ's "resurrection". It is observed with variations of date due to different calendars on the first Sunday after the paschal full moon. It has been a tradition that women wear new clothes on that occasion while children often receive Easter eggs made of chocolate. The egg, originally a Christian symbol of new life, is the main symbol of Easter for most people. Rabbits and chicks are also animals traditionally associated with Easter.

### 1.8.2 Bank Holidays and Public Holidays

The term Bank Holidays dates back to the Bank Holiday Act of 1871 by which banks are closed on the particular days. In Britain, two Bank Holidays are available, one being the Spring Bank Holiday, the other the Summer Bank Holiday. The former happens on the last Monday in May while the latter the last Monday in August, both of which provide British citizens long weekends to enjoy their leisure.

**(1) Boxing Day:** The day after Christmas (December 26th), celebrated as a holiday in parts of the British Commonwealth, when Christmas gifts are traditionally given to service workers or household employees. Nowadays, the Boxing Day has been famous for the commercial products that are sold at a discount, which inspire people's passion to go shopping sometimes like crazy.

**(2) New Year's Day.** England celebrates the New Year from the evening of December 31st into January 1st. Traditionally it is not as widely celebrated as Christmas. More traditionally, on the stroke of midnight, people open the back door (to let the old year out) and ask the first dark-haired man to be seen to come through the front door carrying salt, coal and bread. This means that the following year everyone in the house will have enough to eat (bread), enough money (salt) and be warm enough (coal).

In Scotland they always seem to celebrate New Year better than anywhere else. The celebration of New Year's Eve is called "Hogmanay", which comes from a kind of oat cake that was traditionally given to children on New Year's Eve. In Edinburgh, the celebrations always include a massive party from Prince's Street to the Royal Mile and Edinburgh Castle. Unfortunately, due to overcrowding in the past the event is now ticket only. On New Year's Day (actually from the stroke of midnight), the tradition of first footing is observed. This is because the first person to set foot in a residence in a New Year is thought to profoundly affect the fortunes of everyone who lives there. Generally strangers are thought to bring good luck. Depending on the area, it may be better to have a dark-haired or fair-haired stranger set foot in the house, but it does mean Scotland is a very welcoming place for strangers at New Year!

There are many other minor festivals undoubtedly, such as wedding anniversary, Mother's Day, various saints' days and local customs. Remembrance Day, also called Armistice Day, is worth mentioning. It is an important patriotic festival on November 11th when the British remember the millions of people, both British and foreign, who died in two world wars.

## Notes

[1] **Cumbrian Mountain Range:** It refers to mountains of the Lake District, northwestern England. Scafell Pike (978 m) is the highest point. Studded with lakes and narrow valleys, the range extends through Cumberland, Westmorland, and Northern Lancashire.

[2] **Bristol Channel:** The Bristol Channel is a major inlet in the island of Great Britain, separating South Wales from Devon and Somerset in southwest England, and extending from the lower estuary of the River Severn to the North Atlantic Ocean. It takes its name from the English city of Bristol and is over 30 miles (50 km) across at its widest point.

[3] **Gulf Stream:** The Gulf Stream, together with its northern extension towards Europe, the North Atlantic Drift, is a powerful, warm, and swift Atlantic ocean current that originates in the Gulf of Mexico, exits through the Strait of Florida, and follows the eastern coastlines of the United States and Newfoundland before crossing the Atlantic Ocean.

[4] **Ayrshire:** It is a registration county, and former administrative county in southwest Scotland, located on the shores of the Firth of Clyde. Its principal towns include Ayr, Kilmarnock and Irvine. The town of Troon on the coast has hosted the British Open Golf Championship twice in the last seven years and eight times in total.

[5] **St Augustine:** Augustine of Canterbury (?-604) was a Benedictine monk who became the first Archbishop of Canterbury in the year 598. He is considered the "Apostle to the English" and a founder of the English Church.
