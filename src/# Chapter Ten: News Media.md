# Chapter Ten: News Media

The United Kingdom has a wide diversity of news media with an almost unparalleled number of outlets. If you live in this country, you will find a plentiful of ways to keep yourself well-informed and divert yourself in your leisure time. You can pick up your morning paper at breakfast table, you can turn on the car radio on your way to work, you can enjoy a favorite television show with your family after a tiring day of work, and also you can surf on the Internet as a perfect way to end the day.

## 10.1 The Press

Britain has one of the world's oldest published newspaper industries. Published in the year 1513, *Treve Encountre* is known as the earliest printed newspaper in Britain. In the late 18th and 19th centuries, an increasing number of newspapers began to appear along with the industrialization of economy and the enhanced literary levels due to mass education. *The Observer*, a popular Sunday newspaper, first published in 1791, rendering it the world's oldest national newspaper, while *The Times*, launched in 1785, is Britain's oldest daily newspaper. London's Fleet Street and Manchester remained the center of the newspaper printing and publishing until the 1980s, but now all of the national newspapers have moved their headquarters to other parts of London or away from the capital.

The period from 1860 to 1910 was considered a "golden age" of newspaper publication, with technical advances in printing and communication combined with a professionalization of journalism and the prominence of new owners. Then newspapers became more partisan[1] and there was the rise of new or yellow journalism. Socialist and labor newspapers also proliferated and in 1912 the *Daily Herald* was published as the first daily newspaper of the trade union and labor movement.

There are about 130 daily and Sunday newspapers, over 2,000 weekly newspapers and 7,000 periodical publications in Britain today. The newspapers are traditionally categorized into two types: the quality newspapers (qualities or broadsheets) and popular newspapers (populars or tabloids). Following the tradition of the press of the 18th and 19th centuries, broadsheets are larger in size and are seen as being more intellectual and upmarket. Largely composed of in-depth articles of particular political and social importance, as well as reviews and feature articles about "high culture", the kind of newspapers are generally read by a well-educated, middle-class audience. *The Times*, *The Guardian* and *The Daily Telegraph* are referred to as Britain's "Big Three". Tabloids, on the other side, are smaller in size and seen as being more downmarket than broadsheets, catering to gossip and stories about celebrities, whether in politics, sports or entertainment. They carry lots of crime, sports and sensational human interest stories and thus gain the name "the gutter press". Since the term "tabloid" has become a synonym for down-market newspapers in Britain, some small-format papers which claim a higher standard of journalism refer to themselves as "compact" newspapers instead.

The circulation of newspapers, in relation to the population, is higher in Britain than in any other developed countries except Sweden. About 60 percent of adult Britons read a national newspaper daily, and more than 65 percent buy a Sunday newspaper. Even more Britons read local or regional newspapers.

The press in Britain is at liberty to comment on matters of public interest, subject to law. There is no censorship and it is completely free to say exactly what it likes about anybody or anything, but the publishers can be prosecuted for insult if they deliberately tarnish a person's reputation with fabricated stories. As to political stance, these papers are almost always financially independent of any political party and are not obliged to follow any specific party line.

### 10.1.1 National Daily Newspapers

#### The Times (circulation approximately 692,581)

This is one of Britain's most famous and influential newspapers, and has long been recognized as one of the world's greatest papers. It is still popular among the British people, and they use it for announcements of births, marriages and deaths on the back page, and also for notices of engagement to be married, which are still put on the Court page, arranged in order of state precedence.

*The Times* was founded by John Walter on January 1, 1785 as *The Daily Universal Register*, with Walter in the role of editor. It became *The Times* on January 1, 1788, publishing commercial news and notices, along with some scandals. In 1803, John Walter handed ownership and editorship to his son, John Walter II, whose pioneering endeavors to obtain Continental news, especially from France, helped build the paper's reputation among policy makers and financiers. He also expanded the paper from 4 pages to 12 large pages, and by the time when John Walter III took over the control of the paper in 1848, the foundations of *The Times*'s fame as Britain's prominent national journal and daily historical record had been solidly laid. By the mid-1800s it had become a widely respected influence on British public opinion, and its circulation had increased from 5,000 in 1815 to 40,000 in 1850. A companion weekly with its own editorial staff, *The Sunday Times*, began publishing in 1822. *The Times* was also the first to hire a war correspondent, William Howard Russell, to cover the Crimean War[2].

The paper remained the most significant until around 1860 when a number of more competitive titles appeared, each differentiated by its political biases and interests. It was Sir William Haley, then the director general of the BBC, who made *The Times* once again a great newspaper when he became the editor in the 1950s. In 1966, news was put permanently on its front page in place of advertisements. When taken over by the Thomson group in 1967, it became much less serious than before, and thus gained an increasing circulation. Though the newspaper was printed in broadsheet format for 200 years, it switched to compact size in 2004 in the hope of appealing to younger readers. *The Times* was named as Britain's leading daily newspaper for business people by The British Business Survey 2005, an independent survey sponsored by *The Financial Times*, *The Guardian*, *The Daily Telegraph*, *The Economist*, and *The Times*. In November 2006 *The Times* began printing headlines in its new font, Times Modern.

Renowned for its cautious attitude, *The Times* often reflects the view held by the government. Though politically a moderately centre-right newspaper and traditionally a supporter of the Conservative Party, it supported the Labour Party in the 2001 and 2005 general elections.

#### The Guardian (circulation approximately 358,844)

Founded in 1821 as *The Manchester Guardian* by the non-conformist businessmen, the paper was considered unique among all the other major British newspapers for the arrangements which vested ownership of the undertaking in a trustee (the Scott Trust, via the Guardian Media Group). In 1959, "Manchester" was dropped from the name to reflect its success as a national daily newspaper with an established cosmopolitan reputation.

*The Guardian* once had a reputation as "an organ of the middle class", or in the words of C. P. Scott's son Ted "a paper that will remain bourgeois to the last". But now its stance in politics is described as centre-left or radical, holding a favorable attitude towards the Liberal Party and being rather closer in sympathy to the Labour Party than to the Conservatives.

#### The Daily Telegraph (circulation approximately 1,400,000)

The paper was founded in 1855 with the title *The Daily Telegraph and Courier*, later to be known simply as *The Daily Telegraph*. As the first penny paper in London, it has built a large readership, remaining the market leader for broadsheets, with a circulation of 1,400,000 copies, owing to over 300,000 discount subscribers each day.

A feature of special reporting has been made throughout the paper's history. Its correspondents have covered virtually every major war since the 1860s when the American Civil War burst out.

The year 2004 saw the tenth anniversary of the launch of the Telegraph's website *Electronic Telegraph*. Now re-launched as telegraph.co.uk, the website was the UK's first national newspaper online.

Theoretically independent, the paper has been politically conservative in modern times. According to a MORI survey conducted in 2005, 64% of the *Telegraph* readers intended to support the Conservative Party in the coming elections. The personal links between the paper's editors and the leadership of the Conservative Party, also known by the term Tories, along with the paper's influence over Conservative activists, has resulted in the paper commonly being referred to, especially in *Private Eye*, as the *Torygraph*.

#### The Daily Mirror (circulation approximately 4,400,000)

*The Daily Mirror* was founded in 1903 as a newspaper for women. With the highlight of photo-rich tabloid format, the paper carries many sensational and personal types of stories as well as strip cartoons. The paper is printed in half the normal size and frequently has the largest circulation in the western world. For a period during the 1990s it was renamed *The Mirror*, but reverted to its original name in 2002. It is politically independent and caters for readers who are not much concerned with politics, though sometimes very moderate and sensible comments are made in simple language.

#### The Sun (circulation approximately 7,900,000)

*The Sun* is a daily tabloid newspaper published both in the United Kingdom and Ireland (where it is known as *The Irish Sun*). It was launched by IPC (International Press Corporation) to replace the failing *Daily Herald*, which was committed to the Labour Party. In 1969 *The Sun* was taken over by the tycoon Mr. Rupert Murdoch, who had already bought *The News of the World*, a sensationalist Sunday newspaper.

Politically, *The Sun* remained nominally Labour in the early Murdoch years. It supported Labour in the 1970 general election. However, the paper caused a small stir by changing tack politically, supporting Margaret Thatcher in the 1979 general election. From then on, *The Sun* became an ardent supporter of Margaret Thatcher and her policies. Throughout the 1980s, its stance, on many issues, was to the right of *The Daily Mail*, and it maintained close to the Conservatives when Thatcher was succeeded by John Major in 1990.

In 1978 *The Sun* surpassed *The Daily Mirror* in circulation, partly due to actor Christopher Timothy for the extensive advertising on ITV. Between 1994 and 1996, *The Sun*'s circulation climbed to the peak, with its highest average sale in the week ending July 16, 1994, when the daily figure was 4,305,957. Now its circulation becomes the second highest in the world and the biggest within the UK.

#### Morning Star

*The Morning Star* is a communist British daily newspaper in tabloid format. It is devoted to foreign and domestic news, away from the perceived pro-business stance of other publications. It first appeared in 1930 as the *Daily Worker*, the organ of the Communist Party of Great Britain and the name was changed into *Morning Star* in 1966. The circulation of the *Morning Star* is about 60,000, and has doubled to 150,000 in the past ten years. An online version of the paper was launched on April 1, 2004. Initially only some parts of the site were free, and this policy was changed on January 1, 2009 with all the content freely available now.

### 10.1.2 National Sunday Newspapers

Most Britons enjoy reading daily papers on their breakfast tables, but the dailies have no Sunday editions, which leaves the gap to be filled by Sunday newspapers. Sunday papers also fall into the two categories as broadsheets and tabloids. The broadsheets contain some famous titles like *The Observer* (1791), *The Sunday Times* (1822), *Sunday Telegraph* (1961) and *The Independent on Sunday* (1990), etc., while *News of the World* (1843), *The People* (1881), *Sunday Express* (1918), *Sunday Mirror* (1963) and *The Mail on Sunday* (1982) compose the list of tabloids.

The Sunday press has long been noted for their extremes of good and bad quality. At its best, it may enjoy worldwide renown for their responsible journalism like *The Observer*, which devotes extensive space to the arts, government, education, and politics. At its worst, it can also be as infamous as the *News of the World*, specializing in extensive crime-reports where sex is the focus of interest.

As the leading example of Britain's quality Sundays, *The Observer* was first published on December 4, 1791 by W. S. Bourne, which renders it the world's oldest Sunday newspaper. The paper, reporting on news and background pieces, mostly aims at well-educated readers, including a large international audience. It stands on the same political spectrum as its daily sister paper *The Guardian*, and takes a left-liberal or social democratic line on most issues. There is a visitor centre in London known as The Newsroom, where members of the public are allowed access to the archives of *The Observer* and *The Guardian*, including bound copies of old editions, a photographic library and other items such as diaries, letters and notebooks. The Newsroom also mounts temporary exhibitions and runs an educational program for schools.

The *News of the World*, on the contrary, is the most popular among Britain's Sunday tabloids, and has attracted more readers than any other Sunday paper for more than a century. With an averaging circulation of 3,445,459 copies per week in October 2006, the paper is the best-selling English-language newspaper in the world. One of the factors that can account for the large circulation is its fondness for sex scandals which has won it the nicknames "News of the Screws" and "Screws of the World". Like the other tabloids, the *News of the World* uses aggressive and usually mean-spirited tactics to sell its issues. Also, its allegations about the sexual practices, drug use, or private conduct of celebrities are usually defamatory, which often gets the paper involved in libel actions. England footballer David Beckham and his wife Victoria, for example, brought a legal action against the paper seeking libel damages over an article that carried the headline: "Posh and Becks on the Rocks" in 2005. The legal action was withdrawn in 2006 and "resolved on a confidential basis", according to the couple's spokeswoman Jo Milloy.

In 1990, the British government asked a committee to examine the situation. A published code of practice was drawn up and a new Press Complaints Commission[3] enforced it. According to the code, journalists should not try to obtain information by subterfuge, intimidation or harassment and they should not photograph people without their consent.

### 10.1.3 Periodicals

There are a wide array of different types of periodicals published in Britain, from magisterial quarterlies to coterie art periodicals, from scholarly and professional organs to penny weeklies and illustrated family magazines. Almost every field of study has its own journal published either weekly or monthly. Among them, the most important are *The Economist*, *New Statesman*, *Spectator*.

*The Economist* is a weekly magazine published in London and generally reckoned as one of the world's prominent journals of its kind. It provides world coverage of news and particularly of international and political developments and prospects bearing on the world's economy. Being right-wing in political attitude, the journal has a circulation of about 725,000, with North America accounting for about half of total readership. The *New Statesman* was founded in 1913 as a literary weekly magazine, famous for its aggressive and often satirical analysis of the British and world political scenes. Inclining towards the left wing of the Labour Party, it has become politically an independent socialist forum for serious intellectual discussion, political commentary, and criticism. The *Spectator* is moderately conservative in its editorial stance, and widely noted for its critical reviews and essays on political, literary, and economic issues.

In brief, you can read a variety of newspapers and periodicals in Britain, but their quality can be extremely good or extremely bad. At their worst they can be irresponsible, tasteless and lacking in consideration for individuals. The British press has a great impact on public views and opinions, even the government has sometimes paid money to newspaper publishers to buy their support. While generally speaking the press is "free" from government control and censorship, many British laws limit the freedom of the newspapers. For example, there are laws restraining newspapers from revealing the information about people tried on court because this can prejudice the judicial system. Other laws of libel and defamation prevent the press from printing fabricated stories about people. When it comes to government information, the papers are not allowed to reveal it unless the government says it can be released according to the Official Secrets Act.

## 10.2 Radio and Television

Once playing the most important role in Britons' leisure life, the broadcast media—radio and television—still remains a vital form of entertainment, regardless of the fierce challenge from online media. There are mainly three companies that run the business of radio and television in Britain. They are the British Broadcasting Corporation (BBC), the Independent Television Commission (ITC), and the Radio Authority. Although ultimately accountable to Parliament, the companies have virtually complete independence in the conduct of their business. Of course, the independence is endowed on the premise of certain obligations that the programmes display a proper balance and wide range of subject, impartiality in matters of controversy and accuracy in news coverage, and also the programmes can not pander to vulgar taste.

### 10.2.1 Radio

The British radio market has a history of over 80 years of radio broadcasting and 30 years of commercial radio broadcasting. There are more than 250 radio stations in the United Kingdom, among them, the most prominent are the national networks run by the BBC.

- **BBC Radio 1** broadcasts mostly current pop music output on FM and digital radio, with live music throughout the year.
- **BBC Radio 2** is the UK's most listened-to radio station, with 12.9 million weekly listeners. It features presenters Terry Wogan and Jonathan Ross, and playing popular music from the last 5 decades as well as special interest programmes in the evening.
- **BBC Radio 3** is a classical music station, broadcasting high-quality concerts and performances. At night, it transmits a wide range of jazz and world music.
- **BBC Radio 4** is a current affairs and speech station, with news, debate and radio drama.
- **BBC Radio 5 Live** broadcasts 24 hours live news and sports commentary with phone-in debates and studio guests.

The BBC also provides 40 local radio services, mainly broadcasting a mix of local news and music aimed at an older audience. Also available nationwide are three national commercial channels, namely Absolute Radio, Classic FM and talkSPORT.

Commercial radio licenses are awarded by Office of Communication, a government body which advertises a license for an area and holds a so-called beauty contest to determine which station will be granted permission to broadcast in that area. Most local commercial stations in the United Kingdom broadcast to a city or group of towns within a radius of 20–50 miles. The predominant format is pop music, but many other tastes are also catered for, particularly in London and the larger cities, and on digital radio.

Rather than operating as independent entities, many local radio stations are owned by large radio groups which broadcast a similar format to many areas. The largest operator of radio is Global Radio which bought the former media group, Gcap Media. It owns Classic FM and London's most popular commercial station, 95.8 Capital FM. Other owners are Bauer Radio and UTV Radio, which mainly own stations that broadcast in highly populated city areas.

Many of these stations, including all the BBC stations, are also available via digital television services.

Community radio stations broadcast to a small area, normally within a 3-mile (5-kilometre) radius, and are mostly not-for-profit organizations, owned by local people, on which the broadcasters are volunteers. They are recognized under the Communications Act 2003 as a distinct third tier of radio in the UK. The community radio movement in the UK was founded in the mid-1970s, broadcasting through Restricted Service Licence[4], the Internet and cable.

### 10.2.2 Television

British television broadcasting started in 1932, when the BBC broadcasted its first television programme, which rendered Britain the first country that had a regular daily television schedule directed to homes. They were also the first to have technical professions to work on TVs, though only 9 percent of British households owned a TV during the first four years of its existence. Now it has a collection of free and subscription services over various distribution media, and the Britons who find entertainment in watching TV can switch among almost 600 channels which provide all kinds of programmes.

Television viewing is the most popular leisure pastime in Britain. It is reported that British people spend an average of 4 hours a day watching TV and one of the most frequently read parts of the newspaper is the TV listings. People are allowed access to the latest news and information over the airwaves, and conversations among friends and family will frequently center around programmes that were watched the evening before. So an exclusion from British television viewing may lead to an exclusion from British culture and society as well.

British people watch news, entertainment, sport, sitcoms, drama series and soap operas on TV. Among them, soap operas are one of the most popular genres, which are mostly broadcast during prime time. Unlike American soap operas, which focus on rich, famous and exciting characters, most UK soap operas feature realistic accounts of the daily life of working-class communities. The most popular soaps are *Coronation Street*, *EastEnders*, *Emmerdale*, *Hollyoaks*, *Doctors*, *Neighbours* and *Home and Away*, the first two of which are consistently among the highest-rated shows on British television week-on-week. With its setting located in the northern city of Manchester, *Coronation Street* is the oldest and the most watched show on British television, which began in the 1960s. Its rival, *EastEnders* was first broadcast on BBC 1 on February 19, 1985. Its storylines touch on the domestic and professional lives of the people who live and work in the fictitious east London Borough of Walford. The 1986 Christmas Day episode of *EastEnders* was the highest-rated UK soap opera episode ever, with 30.15 million viewers. This episode was also the most watched program in UK television for the 1980s, compared to the records set by the 1970 splashdown of Apollo 13 (28.6 million viewers), and Princess Diana's funeral in 1997 (32.1 million viewers). The two soaps are renowned as the "flagship", as they are the highest rating programmes for ITV and the BBC respectively.

Daily news and weather forecasts are also popular viewing. The Broadcasting Act[5] is designed to keep ownership of broadcast media widely spread so there are no concentrations of media power in the hands of a few, and foreign ownership (that is ownership outside the European Union) is mainly prohibited. The same legislation also contains safeguards to maintain certain standards of decency, taste, accuracy and balance.

### 10.2.3 The British Broadcasting Corporation

The British Broadcasting Corporation, more familiarly known as the BBC, was founded in 1927 to work with radio and later became involved in TV. With its headquarters located in the Greater London borough of Westminster, the BBC held a monopoly on British television from its inception until 1954 and on radio until 1972.

When it was established in 1922 as a private service radio station, the Corporation was known as the British Broadcasting Company, Ltd. However, the government worried that broadcasting was too important to be left to the market and set up the 1926 Crawford Committee for an enquiry. In his speech delivered to the committee, John Reith (later Lord Reith) stated that "Broadcasting must be conducted in the future as it has been in the past, as a Public Service with definite standards." The next year, upon recommendation of the committee, the company was liquidated and replaced by a public corporation, the British Broadcasting Corporation.

In the early history of the Corporation, John Reith played a vital role as general manager from 1922 and director general from 1927 to 1938. He developed radio broadcasting throughout Great Britain, inaugurated the empire of shortwave broadcasting, and introduced the world's first regular television service in 1936. His concept of public service broadcasting prevailed in Britons and also impacted broadcasting service in many other countries.

British television service was interrupted by World War II, and resumed in 1946. In 1964, a second BBC channel was established, and three years later regular colour television service was introduced by the BBC, which was the first in Europe. It retained the sole broadcasting corporation in Britain until the passage of the Television Act in 1954 and the subsequent creation of a commercial channel run by the Independent Broadcasting Authority in 1955. The BBC's monopoly of radio was broken when the government decided to permit local commercial broadcasting service starting in the early 1970s.

The constitution and finances of the BBC are governed by the Royal Charter, which is reviewed every 10 years. The Charter enables the Queen, with the advice of the Government, to appoint the members of the BBC Trust, an independent 12-member panel, including a chairman, a vice-chairman and separate national governors for Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland. The 2007 Charter specifies that the mission of the Corporation is to "inform, educate and entertain". It claims that the Corporation exists to serve the public interest and to promote its public purposes:

- Sustaining citizenship and civil society;
- Promoting education and learning;
- Stimulating creativity and cultural excellence;
- Representing the UK, its nations, regions and communities;
- Bringing the UK to the world and the world to the UK;
- Helping to deliver to the public the benefit of emerging communications technologies and services, and taking a leading role in the switchover to digital television.

The BBC is run on the basis of television license, which is charged to all owners of equipment capable of receiving live television broadcasts. The level of the fee is determined by the Government under a multi-year agreement with the Corporation. The license fee costs as much as £142.50 per year per household (as of April 2009), but a discount is available for owners with only black-and-white television sets. Because of the revenue from the license, all the BBC channels and its radio are free of any commercial advertisements. The BBC is also closely related to government because of their financial support to the broadcasts, but the station has the independence to decide what to do with money.

The Corporation offers five radio networks and national television channels in Britain, with a variety of programmes ranging from popular music to news and entertainment. Under the Royal Charter, the BBC is required to refrain from broadcasting any opinion of its own on current affairs and issues of public policy and to be disinterested in matters of controversy. Its free-to-air networks include BBC 1 and BBC 2. BBC 1 specializes in shows with broad appeal, such as entertainment, sport, current affairs, drama, and kids shows. BBC 2 caters for audiences with special interest and so broadcasts documentaries and shows geared toward particular social groups, such as the elderly, the handicapped, homosexuals and so on.

Older UK audiences usually refer to the BBC as "the Beeb", a nickname originally used by Peter Sellers in *The Goon Show* in the 1950s. It also has another nickname "Auntie", which is said to originate from the old-fashioned "Auntie knows best" attitude.

Nowadays the BBC is no longer just about TV shows, and it has become instead a multimedia business with wide publishing and educational interests. Not only can you watch BBC shows about cars, pop music, cooking, holidays and gardening, but you can buy magazines which complement the shows by providing further information. It broadcasts in English and 43 other languages all over the world. Its global weekly audience is estimated to be at least 140 million people.

### 10.2.4 The Independent Broadcasting Authority

The Independent Broadcasting Authority (IBA) was the regulatory body in Great Britain for commercial television and commercial/independent radio broadcasts. In this context, "independent" indicated freedom from the BBC, rather than the government. The British television network regulated by the IBA consisted of a band of private companies in competition with the BBC. The IBA came into existence when the Sound Broadcasting Act 1972 endowed the Independent Television Authority responsibility for organizing the new Independent Local Radio (ILR) stations. Governed by the Independent Broadcasting Authority legislation and a license, the IBA was composed of a chairman, a deputy chairman and ten other members appointed by the Home Secretary.

Instead of producing its own radio and television programs, the IBA had all these provided by commercial program companies. Then the main functions of the IBA were to regulate television network, appointing the companies, supervising program arrangements, controlling advertisements and operating transmitting stations.

In the late 1980s the IBA was appointed as regulator and transmitter operator for the first DBS (Direct Broadcasting by Satellite) service for the UK and awarded the franchise to BSB (British Satellite Broadcasting). However, as a part of the Broadcasting Act 1990, it reassigned its regulatory duties of the Independent Broadcasting Authority and Cable Authority to two newly formed bodies, the Independent Television Commission (ITC) and the Radio Authority in 1991.

From January 1, 1991, ITC began its duty of licensing and regulating all non-BBC television services, including ITV and Channel 4 (run by the Channel Four Television Corporation established under the 1990 Act). The ITV network is the oldest commercial television network in Great Britain. It was authorized by act of Parliament in 1954, permitting a single channel to operate by selling airtime to advertisers, which was a quite different way of practice compared with that of the BBC. Since the Broadcasting Act 1990, it has been renamed as Channel 3. The number 3 has no real meaning other than to distinguish it from BBC 1, BBC 2 and Channel 4. Unlike most of the TV channels in the UK, ITV is not owned by one single company, but instead it is operated by four companies which have been licensed to provide regional Channel 3 services. Another national commercial broadcaster is Channel 4, which was first launched in 1982 as a state-owned television channel.

In the early 2000s, the Communications Act 2003 gave rise to a White Paper issued by the British government, declaring the intention to merge the ITC with the Radio Authority, Office of Telecommunications, and Radiocommunications Agency. This merger took effect on December 29, 2003, with most powers of the ITC being taken over by the Office of Communications.

## 10.3 Digital Broadcasting

In the United Kingdom, the development of digital audio broadcasting (DAB) started from the inception of test transmissions by the BBC in 1990. The British digital radio network is unparalleled in that it is the biggest in the world, including 103 transmitters, two national DAB ensembles and forty-eight regional and local DAB ensembles broadcasting over 250 commercial and 34 BBC radio stations across the country.

With the introduction of digital radio technology, many new stations have been launched. Among them, the most influential are several new BBC stations listed as follows.

- **BBC 6 Music** is a music station broadcasting predominantly less mainstream genres of music like alternative rock.
- **BBC 1Xtra** transmits black, urban and gospel music (like rap, RnB and drum'n'bass, etc.).
- **BBC Radio 7** uses the BBC's large archive of speech programming to broadcast classic comedy, drama and children's programming mainly originally from Radio 4.
- **BBC Asian Network** — geared toward the large South Asian community in the UK (a station evolved from BBC Local Radio origins in the 1970s).
- **BBC Radio 5 Live Sports Extra** — a companion to Five Live for additional events.

In addition the BBC World Service is also broadcast nationally on DAB. The BBC's national DAB ensemble broadcasts on frequency block 12B (225.648 MHz) across the country, covering currently 87% of the population of Great Britain. With ongoing procedures, it plans to extend its coverage to 90%.

Digital radios were first sold as car radios in 1997, with a price settled around £800. In Great Britain, 32.1% of the population own a DAB digital radio set and 58% of people are allowed access to digital radio either through a radio set, television or the Internet.

Television broadcasting system also benefits from the digital broadcasting technology. The traditional way of receiving television in the United Kingdom was analogue terrestrial television, however it has now largely been superseded by digital providers. Digital terrestrial television (DTT) was established in Great Britain on November 15, 1998. DTT is usually received by means of a compatible set-top box or Integrated Digital Television (IDTV), connected to an appropriate receiving antenna. In most cases, programmes can be received by using existing aerials originally used for analogue terrestrial television.

The British government licenses the broadcast of channels in six groups, also called multiplexes, labeled 1, 2, A, B, C, and D. The BBC has a multiplex to their own (Multiplex 1), ITV and Channel 4 share Multiplex 2, and Five and S4C share Multiplex A. The remaining spectrum (Multiplexes B, C and D) are auctioned off.

In the digital world, the BBC has started three new television services, known as **BBC Choice** (Established on September 23, 1998, it is the first British TV channel to broadcast exclusively in digital format.), **BBC News 24** (a 24-hour rolling news television channel) and **BBC Parliament** (broadcasts all the work of the parliamentary and legislative bodies of the United Kingdom). Meanwhile, ITV has launched ITV2 in England and Wales, You2 (later UTV2) in Northern Ireland and S2 in Scotland, as well as GMTV2 during the early mornings. ITV2 started from December 7, 1998, broadcasting 24 hours a day as a division of ITV plc. It is available on digital television via satellite, cable, IPTV and terrestrial (Freeview) platforms. UTV2 closed on January 22, 2002, and S2 just over two years after its inception. Both were replaced by ITV2, following their deals with ITV Digital. Channel 4 has expanded greatly after gaining greater independence from the IBA, especially in the multi-channel digital world. Using its space of Multiplex 2, Channel 4 has also set up digital channels like **FilmFour** (a free digital channel screening films), and **E4** which is run as a pay-TV companion to Channel 4. The "E" stands for entertainment, and the channel is mainly geared toward the lucrative group with an average age from 15 to 35. Five sold its half of Multiplex A to the owner of the other half of the multiplex, S4C, who established a subsidiary channel called S4C Digital Networks (SDN).

## 10.4 News Agencies

In the UK, there are several news agencies supplying news reports to organizations in the news trade. Of all these news agencies, Reuters, the Press Association and Extel Financial are the major ones.

### Reuters

Founded by Paul Julius Reuter in 1851, Reuters is one of the leading newswire services in the world. At its very beginning, the company's news service was built up by using electric telegraphy as well as carrier pigeons in its network. Commercial news was the major concern of the company in the early days and its headquarters were located in London to serve banks, brokerage houses and leading business firms. Its first newspaper client was *Morning Advertiser*, a London paper devoted to trade interests. Since the subscription of *Morning Advertiser* in 1858, the Reuters' clientele list has seen a rapid increase of its newspapers clients. The steady expansion of Reuters's newspaper business could be well accounted for by its reputation for being the first to report on news scoops from abroad, like the news of Abraham Lincoln's assassination.

In the 1960s Reuters became one of the first news agencies that use computers to transmit financial news overseas, and in 1973 it began making computer-terminal displays of foreign-exchange rates available to clients. In 1981 Reuters started providing service of making electronic transactions over its network, and it went on to develop a wide range of electronic trading and brokering services. In the quest to secure the company's tradition for objective reporting, Reuters Founders Share Company Limited was formed in 1984 as part of the share float. It held one "Founders Share" which could outvote all other shares to prevent any attempt to alter the rules relating to the Reuters Trust Principles, which set out the company's aim to preserve its independence, integrity, and freedom from bias in its news reporting. In 2008 Reuters incorporated with Canadian electronic publisher Thomson Corporation to form Thomson Reuters. Today the company employs over 10,335 full-time staff and about 13,000 journalists and photographers, operating in at least 200 cities in 94 countries and supplying news text in about 20 languages. The principal news agency in every country in the world is affiliated to Reuters and almost every major news outlet in the world subscribes to the Reuters's services.

Reuters operates under a very strict policy towards upholding objectiveness. In the enforcement of this objective policy, Reuters has been careful not to use the word terrorist except in quotes when major global events are reported. As Stephen Jukes (Reuters global news editor) puts it, "We all know that one man's terrorist is another man's freedom fighter, and that Reuters upholds the principle that we do not use the word terrorist." Of course, this policy of Reuters has caused quite a few unfavorable responses from journalists and newspapers.

### The Press Association

The Press Association is a news agency in the United Kingdom and Ireland, providing London-based service of news-collecting to almost all national and local newspapers, television and radio, as well as many websites. It was founded by provincial newspaper proprietors on a co-operative basis in 1868, consisting two subsidiaries, the Scottish Press Association and the Press Association of Ireland (covering Northern and the Republic of Ireland). Legend has it that they came up with the idea in the back of a Hansom Cab during a traffic jam as a result of London smog. When it was founded the committee who organized it said "The Press Association is formed on the principle of co-operation and can never be worked for individual profit, or become exclusive in its character". Now the PA produces 150 to 200 stories on weekdays, which have a large impact on coverage in UK media. A survey shows that 70% of British news articles in five London newspapers of the most notable quality are largely influenced by the PA version. It is also reckoned a highly reliable source of news by organizations such as the BBC which treats it as "a confirmed, single source". With the development of new technology of media, the company has gone from a news and sport supplier to traditional media, to a service of providing multimedia news, sport, weather and information to thousands of different organizations. A full history of the Press Association was written by Chris Moncrieff, the former Political Editor of the PA in 2001 entitled "Living on a Deadline".

### Extel Financial

Extel Financial is also a London-based news agency and has a network of offices in Europe and the United States and direct representation in Japan and Southeast Asia. It was originally established in 1872 as the Exchange Telegraph company (Extel), with its initial business of laying the first telegraphic cable on the Atlantic seabed to electronically connect London and New York. In the following years, the company gradually grew into one of the leading news agencies, supplying financial information and associated businesses. Extel Cards, first produced in 1922, are one of the notable achievements of the company. The Cards contain brief data on profit and loss, employees, business activities and executive management. Another is the Extel Survey started by Geoffrey Osmint, an independent consultant, in 1974. The Survey focuses on collecting views and votes from fund managers on the services and advice they are getting from research analysts at stock broking houses. The Survey was run independently by Geoffrey Osmint until it became the Extel Survey in 1985.

## 10.5 Online Media

The United Kingdom has been involved with the Internet since the latter's creation. The revolution in information technology (IT) and the introduction of the Internet have resulted in innovations that are having increasingly visible effects not only on the lifestyle of the average Britons, but also on the form of traditional print and broadcast media. With the World Wide Web pages operated by major news organizations, the Internet is quickly becoming the center of news media. Information of all kinds is becoming accessible via the Internet. Instead of picking up a newspaper, or watching the 10 o'clock news, people will log onto the Internet to get the news they want whenever they want it. Many workers listen to the radio through the Internet while sitting at their desk. According to Office of Communication's 2007 report the average UK citizen uses the Internet for 36 minutes every day.

The Britons have cultivated an increasing taste for browsing the websites of the newspapers and magazines whose print editions were most read before. Now almost all of the newspapers and magazines have launched their online editions so as to survive in the fierce competition. Some local newspapers, like the *Southport Reporter*, even go online fully and stop the publication of their printed paper. Local news websites are also emerging in the form of web forums and blog sites. This new trend has reduced the consumption of paper and ink in the UK.

The same trend has become prevailing in the radio industry, as "online" has been regarded as a promised land by most station owners. There are over 350 British radio stations streaming their stations online, not including Restricted Service Licenced radio stations, Hospital radio stations and stations who solely broadcast online. The figures taken by Radio Joint Audience Research (RAJAR) between April and May in 2008 showed that 14.5 million people have listened to radio online, with 9.4 million people listening every week. In 2006, the Phonographic Performance Limited (PPL) proclaimed that it would charge additional royalty fees on British Internet radio stations if they broadcast outside the United Kingdom. In response to this, radio stations which streamed online had to implement IP blocking to prevent listeners outside the UK from listening to their radio programmes and therefore avoided the increased fees. In March 2007, the PPL was reported to re-negotiate royalty fees paid by British Internet radio stations, and the result of the negotiations was that fees would increase.

The BBC launched BBC Online (renamed as bbc.co.uk in 2004) on May 11, 1994 as a paid subscription service. It is a large network of websites including high profile sites such as BBC News and Sport, the video and radio services co-branded BBC iPlayer, the pre-school site Cbeebies, and learning services like Bitesize. Among them, BBC iPlayer was launched in December 2007 as a special service, which allows users to download both radio and TV programmes for up to seven days after broadcast. The television version allows users to either stream programmes or to download them using peer-to-peer and DRM[6] technology. Following its old tradition, the BBC Online was initially entirely free from advertisements, due to its funding derived from compulsory television licence fees from British audiences. However, recently some major online companies have complained that the BBC website receives too much funding from the television license, charging that it is impossible for other websites to compete with the vast range of advertising-free content available on the BBC site.

As an emerging form of news medium, the Internet allows its viewers access to a huge quantity of information, but simultaneously it is also overflowing with news reports at tension with objectivity, information open to question, and even false rumors with harmful effects. Therefore, it is essential for the Internet viewers to take in information online selectively based on their own judgment.

## Conclusion

The function of news media is more than to entertain the readers. Newspapers, radio, television and websites for example, supply Britons with weather forecasts; provide information about political and social issues; carry advertisements; cater for educational programmes; and allow people to express their opinions or seek advice via some interactive columns or programs. What is more important is the role of news media as the watchdog of public interest. It is the obligation of news media to inform the British citizens about issues the country may be facing and what the government is doing to solve these issues. Keeping an eye on the government, the press and broadcast in the UK can secure the objectivity of government reports and parliamentary democracy. This function can be traced in the names of some dominant newspapers, like *The Times*, *The Observer* and *The Guardian*, which are still popular among the British readers today.

Combined with the watchdog function, news media are also endowed with the power of shaping public opinion. As a powerful propaganda tool, the news media may affect the general public's attitudes by focusing attention on some national problems and political issues while ignoring others. Media coverage of an issue increases the national importance that Britons ascribe to it. If the media do, indeed, shape what individuals consider important, they may also alter the bases of judgments about governmental and parliamentary performance. By focusing on some issues and not others, the news may determine the standards by which the governmental and parliamentary performances are evaluated and may, as a result, provoke surges and declines in the popularity of government and parliament.

This powerful persuasive impact of news media on Britons' political attitudes and behavior, according to many analysts, can be misleading and even present a huge threat to liberal democracy. Specifically, when a media bias is involved, it is practically impossible for the public to attain an impartial perception of government and its policies. Some even point out that the power of the press and broadcast has surpassed that of parliament. Contemporary politicians prefer to speak directly to the news media rather than to parliament. They all spend a lot of time and money improving their public image and try to secure favorable coverage on newspapers and TV.

The news media also play a vital role in cultivating a nationwide culture. By reading through their daily papers, tuning on their radio or TV, or surfing online, all Britons may find the same contents, share the same experience and have the same concerns. Regardless of the regional differences and geographical gaps between them, they are the group of people living in the same country, and immersed in the same culture.
